What is the Death Spiral?

Fixed costs remain constant regardless of volume. As output (subscribers/units) decreases, fixed costs must be spread over fewer units — raising the average cost per unit. Higher unit costs reduce competitiveness, driving further volume loss, which raises costs again. This self-reinforcing cycle is the death spiral.

💡 The Death Spiral in Numbers: In FY2021, Peloton had ~2.33M connected fitness subscribers and $4.02B in revenue — roughly $1,726 revenue per subscriber. By FY2024, revenue fell to $2.70B while subscribers declined to ~2.98M (end of FY2024). But fixed operating infrastructure — studios, content production, logistics, G&A — couldn't scale down proportionally. Estimated fixed costs per subscriber rose sharply after the peak, compressing margins and forcing painful restructuring. This is the textbook demand-driven death spiral from your slides.

🎓 Practical Capacity Rate — The Escape Hatch: As your slides note, using a practical capacity overhead rate (based on maximum attainable volume) rather than actual volume can break the spiral. If Peloton had set its subscription pricing based on 3M+ subscriber capacity rather than actual subscribers, it would have isolated the "unused capacity cost" as a separate line item — making the real problem visible to management rather than letting it inflate per-unit costs and trigger further price hikes and churn.
